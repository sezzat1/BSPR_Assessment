Mechanisms of insect-egg induced inter-plant communication

Introduction of the topic and research questions

Plants are chatterboxes1, especially when it comes to communicate a potential threat2 to activate appropriate defense mechanisms3. The inter-plant communication is carried out by a chemical molecule which can be either volatile3 or soil- diffusible4 to reach neighbouring plants.

The systemic acquired resistance (SAR) is a defense mechanism induced in plants by local pathogens infections5 or insect oviposition6. SAR activation also induces a systemic resistance against microbial pathogens7 in the plant to fight bacterial growth in a similar way as the PAMP- triggered immunity8. It has been discovered that the oviposition of the butterfly Pieris brassicae on an Arabidopsis thaliana’s leaf triggers both a broad and local accumulation of salicylic acid (SA) in the leaves, which leads to the SAR mechanism activation6,7. Interestingly, SAR-activated plants seem to be able to relay the signal to uninfected plant, allowing them to mount a SAR response before being attacked9. The exact nature of the molecule carrying the SAR signal from the infected plants to the healthy ones remains elusive. However it has been demonstrated that this compound is not volatile but soil-diffusible9.

Thus it most likely diffuses under the form of a gradient to reach the neighbouring plants. In this case, the distance between the emitting and the receiver plants appears to be a major factor for the intensity of SAR activation in the receiver plant.

In this paper, we will study the effect of distance between A. thaliana P. brassicae’s egg-treated plants (i.e. the sender) and egg-free plants (i.e. the receiver) on the SAR activation intensity in the egg-free plant. To do so, all the plants were inoculated with rifampicin-resistant Pseudomonas syringae and colony forming units (CFU) per cm2 were calculated after incubation. If the SAR activation intensity in the egg-free plant is distance dependent, a decrease of the CFU/cm2 in the egg-free plants should be observed with the reduction of the distance between the egg-infected and the egg-free plant.

Conclusion

The model seems pertinent because we had a significant p-value (p-value = 0.04923 < 0.05). The trend is positive as well (intercept = 7.32964 and slope = 0.08679). As shown in the Figure 3, the correlation is positive and small (0.25), which may be explained by the high standard deviation. A positive correlation exists between the amount of bacteria and distance. Thus, both factors are dependent and one of them could affect the other one.

It could be explained by the fact that the SAR signal promotes a resistance against bacterias in the neighbouring plants, which are not treated with eggs. So when the SAR signal decreases by distance (our results : p-value = 0.0492 < 0.05)m the neighbouring plants are more favourable to develop pathogens like bacterias. In other words, when a plant has insect eggs on its leaves, it triggers the SAR signal. This signal is transmitted more efficiently to the closest egg-free plants than the distant ones.
